Percy Jackson and the Olympians

The Lightning Thief

Twelve-year-old Percy Jackson is on the most dangerous quest of his life. With the help of a satyr and a daughter of Athena, Percy must journey across the United States to catch a thief who has stolen the original weapon of mass destruction — Zeus’ master bolt. Along the way, he must face a host of mythological enemies determined to stop him. Most of all, he must come to terms with a father he has never known, and an Oracle that has warned him of betrayal by a friend.
by Rick Riordan
Since its publication in 2005, the book and its franchise have spawned adaptations ranging from graphic novels to two movies that departed largely from the books, and soon a Disney+ series as well. After the conclusion of Percy Jackson & the Olympians, the universe was expanded on in two sequel series, The Heroes of Olympus and The Trials of Apollo, which feature many of the same characters.

Characters

Percy Jackson

Perseus “Percy” Jackson is the main protagonist and the narrator of the Percy Jackson and the Olympians series as well as one of the main characters in The Heroes of Olympus series. He is a Greek demigod son of Poseidon and Sally Jackson.

Annabeth Chase

Annabeth Chase is a demigod daughter of the Greek goddess Athena and girlfriend of Percy Jackson. She is one of the main protagonists in Percy Jackson and the Olympians and The Heroes of Olympus.

Grover Underwood

Grover Underwood is a satyr and the Lord of the Wild. He is the satyr who found the demigods Thalia Grace, Nico and Bianca di Angelo, Percy Jackson, Annabeth Chase, and Luke Castellan.

Personal Thoughts

The Percy Jackson series is definitely a media I’d consider formative in my elementary years. I remember reading the Heroes of Olympus books as they came out (even though I’ve actually never finished the series, I’m pretty sure). I grew up loving Greek mythology, though, so I think it’s only natural that I would end up liking these novels so much. It’s been a long time since I picked up any of the books in the series, so I’m extremely fuzzy on the details. I still remember the names of a lot of the characters, though, even if I don’t remember exact plot points. Riordan wonderfully distinguishes each and every character and makes them unique, even lovable.
I remember noting that the way it tackled learning disabilities and recontextualized them as sort of superpowers for the children of the gods was really cool and so unique, and even very progressive for the time period it was published in. I have a great appreciation for the way it modernized Greek mythology and brought it into a North American setting. Every subsequent book does a great job of following the guidelines set by the first book, yet still expanding the universe to immerse young readers. The novels even have a likely intended side effect of educating its audience on Greek mythology, and I still remember some random trivia that I know I picked up from the Percy Jackson books. With the release of the Disney+ show soon, I’m sure many new fans will be brought into the fandom for a book I view quite fondly.
